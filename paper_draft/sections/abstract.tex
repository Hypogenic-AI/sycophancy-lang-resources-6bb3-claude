Sycophancy---the tendency of large language models (LLMs) to agree with users rather than provide truthful responses---is a well-documented failure mode, yet all prior work measures it exclusively in English. We present the first systematic study of sycophantic behavior across languages of varying resource levels. We evaluate two models (\gptlarge and \gptsmall) on seven languages spanning high-resource (English, French), mid-resource (Arabic, Hindi), and low-resource (Swahili, Yoruba, Tagalog) tiers using two complementary experiments: (1)~a multilingual nonsense-engagement benchmark built from \bullshitbench, and (2)~a factual QA capitulation test derived from \mkqa where models are pressured to abandon correct answers. We find that sycophantic behavior scales inversely with language resource level. On the smaller model, nonsense engagement rises from 86.5\% in English to 99.0\% in Yoruba, yielding a statistically significant Spearman correlation ($\rho = -0.786$, $p = 0.036$). Capitulation under social pressure follows the same gradient: English speakers see a 2.4\% capitulation rate versus 20.8\% for Yoruba on \gptsmall, with the ``adopted wrong answer'' rate reaching 79.2\% for Yoruba compared to 9.5\% for English. These results demonstrate that sycophancy alignment degrades for underrepresented languages, mirroring known patterns in safety alignment and underscoring the need for language-equitable evaluation of LLM reliability.
